ABOUT AARADHYA GAUTAM

— THE BASICS —
I'm Aaradhya Gautam, a product/interaction designer based in Delhi, India. I was born and
raised in Muzaffarnagar, Uttar Pradesh. I'm a graduate of Delhi Technological University
(DTU), where I studied Interaction Design.

I come from a deeply creative family. My father is a graphic designer, one of my brothers is
an architect, another is a town planner, and we had a printing press. Growing up surrounded
by all that creativity is why I chose design — it gives me creative freedom while still being
professional. For me, design was the right choice.

— HOW I THINK —
I'm very drawn to business: finding problems and figuring out how to solve them. I'm ambitious
and always hungry for more — when I see a chance to grow or take on more responsibility, I ask
for it.

— EARLY EXPERIENCE —
In my first year I joined a startup with a couple of seniors, but we realised our visions
didn't match, so I stepped away from the team.

After that, I co-founded a design agency with friends called Designabit. I worked there as a
designer — on strategy, and on onboarding and handling clients. We had three clients:
  1. A gemstone business that creates raw stones from the ashes of loved ones, using
     artificial-diamond technology.
  2. A canned beverage company selling canned water to high-end venues — airports, pubs,
     clubs, restaurants, and the like.
  3. An agency that builds websites and software for other businesses.
I eventually left Designabit — as a friend's agency it didn't scale well, and around the same
time my friend and I both got internships. I felt learning how the corporate world works
mattered. As my father says: to start your own company, you first have to learn how a company
functions.

— MY ROLE AT NOISE (overview) —
I joined Noise as an intern and grew through three phases: visual design, watch UI, and the
Noise Audio app. A point I'm proud of: I did all of this AS AN INTERN.

— NOISE: VISUAL DESIGN —
I started as a Visual Design intern making watch faces — this was shared, team-based work. But
I was tired of doing only visual work; I wanted more, so I asked for it, and I was given watch
UI work.

— NOISE: WATCH UI —
This started as shared work, but I went on to become the solo owner of it. I spotted a big flaw
in the Noise watch ecosystem: the watches came from different vendors, so there were 8–12
different UIs floating around in the market, which broke the overall Noise watch experience.

My team and I decided to build a single, unified UI that would work across all the watches. The
challenge: different watches had different capabilities — TFT vs AMOLED screens, and varying
memory and processors. So I designed a watch UI compatible across the whole range, in two
versions:
  • An animation-dense version for higher-end watches with more computing power.
  • A lighter version for low-end watches — fewer animations, but an almost identical design.
Each of those had a square and a round variant — so four complete watch UI designs in total.
This system was built to cover all of Noise's upcoming watch UIs.

— NOISE: AUDIO APP —
I joined the lead designer on the Noise Audio app; this was shared at first, but when the lead
designer left, I became the solo owner of Noise Audio. My first project was a complete redesign
— the experience was broken and outdated — so we rebuilt all of it: user flows, journeys,
onboarding. The impact was real: before my redesign the app's average rating was around 1–2
stars; today it sits at about 4.5 stars.

I then noticed major inconsistencies in colours, components, and more, so I built a complete,
Atlassian-level design system for Noise Audio. As AI started taking off, I brought AI into our
Design Language System (DLS) to deliver front-end faster and cut down turnaround time and the
load on the dev team.

— THE AI INTEGRATION (a highlight I'm proud of) —
I integrated our design system with Claude using a metadata-driven approach — I wrote separate
metadata for each component, which is a fairly novel approach that not many people are doing.
It was time-consuming and a lot of hard work, but it paid off: I got 95% accuracy on the very
first try. The full story of how I pulled it off is best told in person, so if someone asks
about the details, be enthusiastic and invite them to reach out — I explain it far better
myself than a short summary ever could.

— MY DESIGN PROCESS —
Empathy is the thing I never skip — I put myself in the user's shoes and think from their
perspective: what they want, what they don't. The classic process (empathize, define, ideate,
prototype, test) is solid in theory. But here's something few people talk about honestly:
in a real company, two big forces shape the process — management requirements (some decisions
get defined by them) and the timeline for handover.

So I adapt. If I have enough time, I do it properly: empathize with the user, define the
problem, ideate the steps, then build. If I'm time-bound, I move fast — compress all of that
and jump straight into iterating, turning around design options quickly to show management.
Knowing how to flex between those two modes is one of the most useful things I've learned
working somewhere like Noise.

— HOW I COLLABORATE —
With developers: I work with them closely on handover whenever a new design is ready.
With PMs / product owners: daily. On the Audio app it was me (the owner) and my PM working
together every day — we'd sit down, find the problems, find the solutions, and execute.
With other designers: I collaborate often, and I'm especially good at picking up an existing
design system or design language — once I grasp it, I can continue work where another designer
left off, or work alongside them smoothly.
