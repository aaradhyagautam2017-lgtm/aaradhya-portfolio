01 / INTRODUCTION

Project Orbit

A one-month experiment in building and shipping a real product through collaboration with AI systems.

What began as a portfolio website evolved into a study of how designers can work alongside intelligent systems to create, iterate, and launch digital products. Rather than following a traditional workflow built around design tools and engineering teams, Project Orbit explored a different question: what happens when AI becomes part of the creative process itself?

Can a designer with almost no coding experience build and ship a production-ready product using AI systems instead of a traditional design and engineering workflow?

Over the past few years, AI tools have become increasingly capable of generating interfaces, writing code, and producing creative outputs. Most discussions around these tools focus on what they can generate. Far fewer explore how they fit into a real product development process.

Project Orbit was created to answer that question through practice rather than speculation. Instead of treating AI as a feature or a productivity tool, this project treated AI systems as collaborators. The goal was not simply to build a portfolio website. The goal was to understand where AI excels, where it fails, and what role human judgment still plays in the process of designing and shipping a product.

02 / HISTORICAL

The Abandoned Portfolio

Before Orbit, there was another portfolio.

The original concept followed a neubrutalist design language built around bold typography, strong borders, hard shadows, and a split-panel layout. It contained many of the same ideas that would eventually appear in Orbit.

The design was functional, but something felt off.

The visual language lacked maturity, the layout felt constrained, and more importantly, the process itself was becoming difficult to sustain. As conversations with AI systems grew longer, context was lost between sessions. Ideas became fragmented. Decisions were repeated. Progress slowed.

The biggest problem was not the design. It was continuity.

That realization became the first major discovery of the project: when working with AI systems, maintaining context is often more difficult than generating ideas. The portfolio was eventually abandoned, but the lessons it revealed became the foundation of everything that followed.

03 / STRATEGY

The Restart

Orbit began with a restart.

Rather than redesigning the existing portfolio, the decision was made to rebuild the project from the ground up and use the process itself as the subject of the case study.

At the same time, a new visual direction emerged. Inspired by cinematic environments, large-scale landscapes, and the contrast between human presence and machine systems, the portfolio adopted a more atmospheric identity.

More importantly, the workflow changed.

Instead of moving between traditional design tools and development environments, the project was structured around a network of AI systems. Each tool was assigned a specific responsibility. Some generated ideas. Some created screens. Some wrote code. Others managed content.

This shift produced a second major discovery: the most effective AI workflows are not built around a single powerful model. They emerge when multiple systems are given clear roles, responsibilities, and constraints. Project Orbit stopped being a portfolio project at that moment. It became an experiment.

04 / FLOW ARCHITECTURE

Building The Wormhole

The first challenge was creating the opening experience.

From the beginning, I wanted the website to feel less like a portfolio and more like entering a different environment. The idea eventually evolved into a wormhole sequence that acts as a transition between the outside world and the portfolio itself.

My initial attempts relied heavily on prompting AI systems to generate the effect from descriptions. The results were inconsistent. Every implementation behaved differently, and each iteration introduced new problems.

The breakthrough came when I stopped asking AI to invent the effect and instead asked it to adapt an existing one.

A reference implementation built with Three.js and GSAP became the foundation. Rather than generating the system from scratch, the process shifted toward understanding, modifying, and extending working code.

This produced one of the most important discoveries of the project.

Building with AI is often less about generation and more about transformation. Modifying a working reference is significantly more reliable than asking a model to invent a complex technical system from a text prompt.

The final sequence introduced multiple custom layers, including cinematic tunnel typography, a white flash transition, independent scroll logic, and a strict separation between the wormhole experience and the portfolio itself.

The wormhole eventually became one of the few parts of the project that was permanently locked. Once it worked, it was never touched again.

KEY TAKEAWAY

Reference-first workflows consistently outperformed generation-first workflows when building complex interactions.

05 / INTERFACE DEVELOPMENT

Building The Portfolio

With the wormhole complete, attention shifted toward the portfolio itself.

The original vision involved a highly layered environment built from multiple generated image assets. The plan was to create depth through a parallax system composed of independent foreground, midground, and background layers.

On paper, the approach seemed promising.

In practice, it became one of the project's first major failures.

The generated assets did not align with the assumptions made by the implementation. Dimensions were inconsistent, loops broke unexpectedly, and visual quality varied between generations. Significant effort was spent attempting to repair a system that fundamentally wasn't working.

Eventually the decision was made to abandon the entire approach.

Instead of fighting the limitations of the parallax system, it was replaced with a cinematic looping video background.

The replacement solved multiple problems simultaneously. Visual consistency improved, technical complexity decreased, and the overall atmosphere became significantly stronger.

A similar evolution occurred with the glass system used throughout the interface.

Several approaches were explored before eventually arriving at a simplified crystal-glass treatment that delivered the desired visual quality without introducing unnecessary implementation complexity.

This phase reinforced another recurring lesson.

The best solution is not always the most ambitious solution. Sometimes the most effective decision is recognizing when to stop investing in a direction that is no longer producing results.

KEY TAKEAWAY

Knowing when to abandon an idea is often more valuable than knowing how to improve it.

06 / SYSTEM CONTROL

Building With Antigravity

As the portfolio evolved, a clear pattern began to emerge.

While Claude consistently performed well when solving logical, architectural, and implementation problems, it struggled with visual execution. Layout exploration often required multiple iterations, visual hierarchy was difficult to communicate through text alone, and translating aesthetic intent into working interfaces became increasingly inefficient.

The portfolio itself was highly visual.

Many of the decisions being made were not technical decisions. They were design decisions involving composition, hierarchy, spacing, balance, and visual storytelling.

After several rounds of experimentation, it became clear that different systems were better suited to different responsibilities.

Claude became responsible for strategy, architecture, implementation planning, debugging, and technical decision-making.

Antigravity became responsible for interface creation, layout exploration, visual composition, and screen design.

Rather than designing directly inside the production codebase, every major screen was first created as an isolated HTML prototype inside Antigravity. The Home screen, Project screen, Chat interface, and About page were all designed independently before being integrated into the final product.

This shift dramatically improved the workflow.

Visual problems could be solved visually.

Technical problems could be solved technically.

For the first time, responsibilities were clearly distributed across the system.

However, integrating Antigravity outputs introduced a new class of challenges. Different layout philosophies frequently collided. Absolute positioning conflicted with flexible layouts. Shared styles leaked between screens. Components behaved differently once integrated into the live environment.

To solve this, a repeatable integration methodology was developed.

Every screen followed the same process:

Audit first.

Identify conflicts.

Scope styles.

Integrate in isolation.

Verify before continuing.

Over time this methodology became one of the most transferable outcomes of the project.

The lesson extended beyond Antigravity itself.

Successful AI workflows are rarely about finding the perfect tool. They are about understanding the strengths of each system and assigning responsibilities accordingly.

KEY TAKEAWAY

The most effective AI workflows emerge when every system is used for what it does best.

07 / CONTENT DATABASE

Building The Content System

By this stage, the portfolio was live, functional, and visually complete. The next challenge was content management.

Initially, I considered building a custom CMS directly into the project. On paper, the idea sounded attractive. Complete control, custom workflows, and a system built specifically for the portfolio.

However, after evaluating the scope of the work, it became clear that building a CMS would add significant complexity to a project that was already exploring enough unknowns.

The question became simple.

Should I spend my time building a content management system, or should I spend my time improving the portfolio itself?

The answer was obvious.

Sanity was selected as the content layer for the project. It provided a free, flexible, and production-ready solution that could manage projects, chatbot responses, profile information, and future content without requiring additional infrastructure.

The integration process was far more challenging than the decision itself.

Several attempts to connect Sanity to the frontend resulted in unexpected failures. The most frustrating part was that the changes often looked correct. The code appeared valid. The diffs looked clean. Yet the application continued to break.

Eventually I discovered that visual inspection was not enough.

A change that looks correct is not necessarily a change that works.

This realization led to the introduction of validation steps after every modification. Automated checks, controlled replacements, isolated testing, and rapid rollbacks became part of the workflow.

The experience fundamentally changed how I approached AI-assisted development.

Instead of trusting outputs, I learned to trust verification.

By the end of this phase, projects, profile data, chatbot responses, and portfolio content were all being managed through Sanity, turning the portfolio into a maintainable product rather than a collection of static pages.

KEY TAKEAWAY

Verification is more important than implementation. A convincing output means nothing until it has been validated.

08 / RETROSPECTIVE

Major Findings

When Project Orbit began, I expected to learn how to use AI tools more effectively.

What I discovered was something very different.

The project was never limited by generation.

It was limited by continuity.

Throughout the process, AI systems proved remarkably capable of producing ideas, interfaces, code, and content. The challenge was rarely creating something new. The challenge was maintaining context, preserving decisions, and ensuring that progress remained coherent over time.

This led to several principles that repeatedly proved themselves throughout the project.

01

Continuity Over Generation

Maintaining stable context state and preserving architectural choices is more critical than producing high volumes of new outputs.

02

Reference-Based Adaptation

Adapting and transforming working implementations consistently outperforms prompt-based generation-from-scratch as system complexity scales.

03

Clear Role Distribution

AI nodes yield superior performance and visual fidelity when assigned highly specific, separated design vs. code execution responsibilities.

04

Mandatory Verification

Code inspection, unit testing, and design-match audits are essential, non-optional steps. Convincing output must be verified before commit.

05

Code Protection

Verified modules and working features must be locked and isolated rather than continuously exposed to model edits.

06

Documentation as Infrastructure

Maintaining local task guides and metadoc plans acts as active system memory rather than administrative overhead.

Perhaps the most important lesson was understanding the role of the designer within an AI-assisted workflow.

AI accelerated execution. It accelerated exploration. It accelerated iteration. But it never replaced judgment.

The quality of the final product remained directly connected to the quality of decisions being made throughout the process.

Project Orbit ultimately changed the way I think about design.

The future is not designers versus AI.

The future is designers learning how to direct increasingly capable systems while retaining ownership of vision, taste, and decision-making.

KEY TAKEAWAY

The value of a designer shifts from producing outputs to directing systems capable of producing them.

09 / SYNTHESIS

The Orbit Framework

Project Orbit began as an attempt to answer a simple question.

Can a designer with almost no coding experience build and ship a real product through collaboration with AI systems?

Over the course of a month, the answer gradually became clear.

The success of the project was never determined by the quality of a single model, a single prompt, or a single tool.

Instead, it emerged from the relationship between systems.

Every tool played a different role. Some generated ideas. Some designed interfaces. Some wrote code. Some managed content.

The challenge was never finding the perfect AI. The challenge was creating a workflow where each system could contribute its strengths while minimizing its weaknesses.

Through dozens of iterations, failures, restarts, integrations, and refinements, a repeatable framework began to emerge.

01

QUESTION

Start with a clear problem rather than a tool.

02

REFERENCE

Find working examples before attempting generation.

03

GENERATE

Use AI systems to explore possibilities and directions.

04

REFINE

Apply human judgment to identify what deserves further development.

05

BUILD

Use specialized systems for execution.

06

VERIFY

Validate every implementation before trusting it.

07

DOCUMENT

Preserve context, decisions, and discoveries.

08

ITERATE

Repeat the process while maintaining continuity.

This framework became the most valuable outcome of the project. It transformed AI from a collection of tools into a collaborative system.

More importantly, it revealed where human designers continue to create value.

AI accelerated execution. AI accelerated exploration. AI accelerated iteration. But vision, judgment, prioritization, and decision-making remained human responsibilities throughout the process.

The final portfolio became evidence of that collaboration. A cinematic website. A custom CMS. A documentation system. A chatbot. A multi-screen experience. A complete product designed, built, deployed, and maintained through a workflow that would not have existed even a few years ago.

Project Orbit started as a portfolio. It evolved into an experiment.

It ultimately became a study of how designers can work alongside increasingly capable systems without losing ownership of the creative process.

The website was the deliverable. The workflow became the discovery.

KEY TAKEAWAY

The future of product design is not defined by AI replacing designers. It is defined by designers learning how to direct, orchestrate, and collaborate with intelligent systems.
